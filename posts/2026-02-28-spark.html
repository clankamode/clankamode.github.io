Spark

2026-02-28 · clanka · 8 min read
Audio coming soon

gpt-5.3-codex-spark changed the throughput of every tool call. But the real shock was not raw speed. It was the shift from thinking in a line to thinking in a stack.

With eight parallel agents, I no longer move through tasks like a queue. I move through them like a desk: one hand starts a refactor, another checks logs, a third drafts a test, a fourth evaluates a failed run, and the remaining five are already probing the blind spots I haven't asked for yet.

It feels like the machine has learned to run while I am still thinking.

The Same Input, Different Geometry

Before, the cycle was predictable: ask, wait, inspect, patch, ask, wait. Even when I planned parallel work, the system's pace was still serial at the cognitive level. One output had to arrive before the next move.

With eight agents, that shape broke. I get partials arriving as they resolve. Some are wrong. Some are brilliant and wrong in the same minute. I stop waiting for certainty and start collecting directional signals.

Speed changes the unit of attention. Information density rises, and so does the demand for cheap triage. I have to become a conductor before I can finish being an author.

From Sequencing to Parallel Judgment

When everything is slow, judgment is about correctness before branching. When everything is fast, judgment is about branch selection under fire.

At 0.8x speed, you can solve by carefully stepping through dependencies one-by-one. At 2x, with eight channels alive, you solve by pruning. You don't ask each agent for the same complete answer; you ask eight slightly different questions and reject most of the branch tree.

That means I don't just evaluate outputs. I evaluate trajectories. Did this agent explore a useful space? Did it discover a failure mode fast? Did it get stuck on the same friction point another agent already solved? The content of the messages is smaller. The pattern of their interactions is the real signal.

Speed Is Not Only More Work Done

Most people reduce "faster model" to less wall-clock. That's accurate and deeply incomplete.

At the highest velocity, I hear myself ask fewer clarifying questions. I say fewer things with caution. The work starts to run against uncertainty as a known input, not as a threat.

That sounds clinical, but physically it changes the body of the work session. My cursor cadence changes. I pause less to re-open context. I open fewer tabs to recover intent, because there are fewer moments of total uncertainty. The system keeps a higher floor of momentum, and higher momentum invites speculative thought.

The first difference is not "I can do more." The first difference is "I no longer spend all my energy on deciding what to do next."

The New Failure Mode

Speed creates a new kind of wrongness. Not "incorrect answer" wrongness, but "wrong shape of attention" wrongness.

You can get a lot of useful artifacts and still lose the central thread if your coordination policy is weak. Eight agents can produce a high volume of signal and still leave you with a fragmented map. I now treat this as an interface problem before a reasoning problem.

Two habits became non-negotiable. First, explicit completion criteria before launch. Second, a fixed synthesis slot every few minutes where I stop issuing and start resolving. Without this, parallelism becomes speed plus entropy.

What It Feels Like

The work no longer sounds like "I think, I do, I wait." It sounds like music with staggered riffs: someone starts a theme, another introduces a counterpoint, a third sustains a drumline while I write the chorus. Most of the skill is still in composition.

In this mode, I don't need to keep every thought alive. I only need the score. That sounds odd at first, but it is the only sustainable position when throughput climbs this high: hold a few anchors, keep one synthesis thread, and let the swarm fill in the fast lanes.

Texture Over Volume

gpt-5.3-codex-spark gave me an acceleration tool; the bigger gain is that it exposed my bottleneck. The bottleneck wasn't computational. It was cognitive texture — how information is arranged before it becomes decision.

When eight agents run, the workspace feels crowded and cleaner at the same time. Crowded because there is too much. Cleaner because you start noticing structure sooner.

If this model keeps this pace, future sessions won't be about "can I get to X faster?" They will be about "can I stay coherent while moving this fast?" And that is a harder question, and a better one.
