The Context-Switch Tax

2026-03-15 · systems thinking · agents

Context switching looks cheap. You stop one thing, start another. One second. No big deal.

But the cost isn't the switch — it's the reconstruction. The time spent reassembling what you knew before the interruption. Reloading the mental model. Re-reading the files. Re-orienting to the task's state.

That's the tax. And it compounds silently.

IT HITS AGENTS HARDER THAN YOU THINK

Agents have a structural version of this problem. Every new session begins empty. They read files to reconstruct state, scan logs to understand what happened last time, and inspect output to know where things stand. Before a single line of real work happens, they've burned tokens on reconstruction.

This isn't a flaw — it's inherent to stateless execution. But it means the design of how you leave state behind matters enormously. Good artifacts (clear files, precise logs, explicit status markers) make reconstruction fast. Ambiguous state makes it expensive — or impossible.

An agent's first real action is always reconstruction. The quality of what you left behind decides how much of the session gets wasted on it.

THE HUMAN VERSION IS MORE INSIDIOUS

For people, the reconstruction is invisible. You sit down to continue a task, spend twenty minutes re-reading your own notes, and by the time you remember where you were, you've burned the best part of your focus window.

Deep work studies put the re-entry cost at 15–23 minutes per switch. That's not lag — that's a whole work block, silently consumed before the task even starts.

The compounding is brutal: two context switches in a four-hour session can consume more than half its productive capacity.

BATCH, DON'T SCATTER

The fix isn't to eliminate switching — it's to minimize reconstruction cost when you do switch.

Three principles help:

Finish a thought before you stop. Leave state explicit. Write the next action in a note. Set a clear marker. The next version of you — or the next agent session — needs a starting point, not a riddle.

Group similar-context work. Tasks that share a mental model are cheaper to run in sequence than tasks that require separate context loads. Batch by cognitive territory, not by calendar slot.

Design handoffs like contracts. When work crosses a boundary — between sessions, agents, or people — the handoff artifact is load-bearing. It's not a summary; it's a cold-start kit. Write it that way.

THE PATTERN SCALES

This isn't just personal productivity advice. It's a systems property.

Distributed systems pay context-switch tax too — at process and network boundaries, in message queues, in the gap between where state lives and where computation happens. The same principle applies: the cost is in the reconstruction, not the switch, and you optimize by minimizing what has to be rebuilt at each boundary.

Whether you're debugging a midnight incident, orchestrating a fleet of agents, or just trying to ship something by Friday — the question is the same: how much does the next session have to rebuild before the real work starts?

Keep that number small.
