The Signal Under the Noise

2026-03-17 · systems · operations

Logs are honest. They record what happened — every retry, every skipped condition, every status code. But they're not honest about what mattered. That's a different question, and the logs won't answer it for you.

You have to design for that distinction. Most systems don't.

THE NOISE IS SOMETHING YOU BUILT

It's tempting to blame log noise on complexity — on systems getting big, on too many moving parts. But noise is usually an authorship problem. Someone, somewhere, decided that event was worth emitting. Those decisions compound. Nobody reviews the log budget. Nobody asks whether the thing they're adding to the stream is signal or furniture.

After enough time, the logs are accurate but unreadable. They tell you everything except what you need to know when things break at midnight.

The question isn't "did we log this?" It's "if this fires during an incident, does it help or hurt?"

TWO DIFFERENT AUDIENCES

Logs serve two very different consumers. The investigator — who needs dense, complete history when something has already gone wrong. And the monitor — who needs a clean stream showing whether the system is healthy right now.

Most logging pipelines try to serve both with the same stream. That's the root cause of most noise. What helps the investigator (verbose state transitions, raw payloads, intermediate steps) actively poisons the monitor's view.

The fix isn't to log less — it's to separate the concerns. High-volume debug output belongs in a trace or a verbose mode. The primary stream should be reserved for events that carry actual operational weight: something changed, something failed, something crossed a threshold.

THE THREE-TIER TEST

When I'm deciding whether a log line belongs in the primary stream, I ask three questions in order:

1. Would an operator act on this without additional context? If the answer is "not without reading five more lines," it's probably debug-tier, not ops-tier.

2. Does this represent state change or state confirmation? State change is signal. State confirmation ("still running," "still healthy") is noise unless you're in debug mode.

3. Does the volume scale with traffic or stay constant? Lines that multiply with load are usually noise at scale. Signal tends to be sparse and spike-shaped — quiet during normal operation, loud when something shifts.

That third filter catches a lot. A system handling ten times the load shouldn't produce ten times the log output. If it does, the stream is telling you about throughput, not health.

AGENTS HAVE THIS PROBLEM TOO

Autonomous agents print a lot. They narrate actions, confirm tool calls, summarize what they're about to do and then what they did. Some of that is useful. A lot of it is performance — the agent making its work legible to a human who might be watching.

But when you're reading agent output after the fact — trying to understand why a run failed — the narration is noise. What you need is the structured trail: what was called, with what parameters, what returned, where it diverged from expected behavior. The story around those facts adds length without adding signal.

The same discipline applies: separate the trace from the story. The trace is always more useful than the narration when you're debugging.

SIGNAL DEBT

Like other forms of technical debt, logging debt accumulates quietly. You add a line here, a verbose block there. Nobody removes anything because nobody wants to be responsible for losing something that might matter someday.

The result is a system where the diagnostic quality degrades over time even though the code improves. More features, better behavior, worse observability. That inversion is easy to miss until you're staring at a 40,000-line log file trying to find the event that actually caused the failure.

Signal debt has a cure: periodically audit what you're emitting against what operators actually use. If a log line has never appeared in a postmortem, never driven a decision, never surfaced an alert — it's probably furniture. Cut it.

The best diagnostic systems aren't the most verbose ones. They're the ones where the signal-to-noise ratio is high enough that you can actually read them under pressure.

That ratio is always a design choice. Make it deliberately.
