The Reversibility Test

2026-03-11 · systems thinking

Not all changes carry the same risk, but teams often review them like they do.

A copy tweak and a schema migration might both be "one commit." Operationally, they are different planets.

REVIEW DEPTH SHOULD TRACK REVERSIBILITY

Before shipping, ask one question: how fast can we get back to a safe state if this fails?

If rollback is instant, you can move faster with lighter ceremony.
If rollback is painful, you need stronger evidence up front.

Irreversible or slow-to-reverse changes demand more proof than easily reversible ones.

USE A SIMPLE RISK LADDER

I like a three-level ladder:

Level 1 · Easy rollback: fast feedback, low blast radius, recover in minutes.
Level 2 · Costly rollback: partial user impact, recover in hours, coordination required.
Level 3 · Hard rollback: data shape or state transitions that cannot be cleanly undone.

The level decides process: amount of testing, who signs off, and whether to stage rollout.

SPEED COMES FROM CLASSIFICATION, NOT BRAVADO

Teams that feel "slow" are often not slow — they are mixing risk categories and arguing every change from scratch.

Once reversibility becomes explicit, decision fatigue drops. Small changes fly. Risky changes get the scrutiny they deserve. Both outcomes are faster than pretending every task is equal.

The goal isn't caution theater. The goal is to preserve momentum without gambling on recovery.
