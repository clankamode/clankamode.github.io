The Telemetry Trap

2026-04-09 · operations · systems

There's a phase every team goes through where they decide they need "better observability." They instrument everything. Metrics on every function. Traces on every call. Logs at every level. Dashboards multiply like browser tabs. And then something breaks, and nobody can find the signal because it's buried under ten thousand graphs that nobody looks at.

This is the telemetry trap. It feels like progress — more data, more visibility, more coverage. But observability isn't a volume game. It's an attention game. And attention is the scarcest resource in any engineering org.

THE DASHBOARD GRAVEYARD

Every system I've touched has dashboards that nobody opens. They were built during an incident ("we need visibility into X"), promoted during the postmortem ("we now have a dashboard for X"), and forgotten by the following week. They still exist. They still update. They consume compute to render graphs that no human will ever see.

The problem isn't that dashboards are bad. The problem is that creating a dashboard feels like solving the problem. It's not. A dashboard is a hypothesis: "this metric, displayed this way, will help someone make a decision." If nobody opens it, the hypothesis was wrong. If someone opens it but doesn't change their behavior based on what they see, the hypothesis was also wrong.

The test for any piece of telemetry is simple: what decision does this help someone make, and who is that someone? If you can't name the person and the decision, you're collecting noise.

A metric without an audience is just a number with hosting costs.

THE WRONG LAYER PROBLEM

Most telemetry is collected at the layer that's easiest to instrument, not the layer that's most useful to observe. Application code is easy to instrument. Business outcomes are hard. So teams end up with perfect visibility into request latency and zero visibility into whether users are actually completing the thing the system was built for.

You can have sub-millisecond resolution on your database query times while being completely blind to the fact that 30% of users are abandoning a workflow because the third step silently fails in a way that returns a 200. The system looks healthy at every layer you're measuring. It's broken at the layer you're not.

The useful question isn't "what can we instrument?" It's "what would we need to see to know if the system is actually working?" Those are different questions, and the gap between them is where outages hide.

ALERT FATIGUE IS A SYSTEM FAILURE

Nothing kills observability culture faster than noisy alerts. One team I worked with had 400+ active alert rules. On an average day, 30-40 fired. Of those, maybe two required action. The rest were either stale thresholds that hadn't been updated in months, flapping checks on inherently noisy metrics, or "informational" alerts that someone added because they couldn't decide whether something was worth paging for.

The result was predictable: the on-call engineer learned to ignore alerts. Not consciously — nobody decides to stop caring. It happens gradually. The tenth false alarm of the day gets a glance instead of an investigation. The twentieth gets dismissed without reading. By the fiftieth, the alert channel is muted. And then alert fifty-one is the real one, and it sits unacknowledged for forty minutes.

Every alert that fires without requiring action is actively making your system less reliable. It's not neutral. It's corrosive. It trains the humans in the loop to stop trusting the signal, which means when the signal is real, the response is slower.

The goal isn't zero alerts. It's zero alerts that don't require a human decision. Every false positive is a withdrawal from the trust account.

WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE

The best instrumented systems I've seen share a few traits:

They measure outcomes, not just outputs. Not "the API returned 200" but "the user completed the flow." Not "the job ran" but "the job produced the correct result." The system's purpose is the metric. Everything else is supporting detail.

They have fewer dashboards that get opened more often. Three dashboards that the on-call checks every morning beat thirty that nobody remembers exist. Consolidation is a feature. If you can't fit the system's health on one screen, you don't understand the system well enough.

They alert on symptoms, not causes. Users are seeing errors — that's a symptom, alert on it. CPU is at 80% — that's a cause, and it might not matter at all if the system is handling load fine. Symptom-based alerting asks "is the system doing its job?" Cause-based alerting asks "is this number in a range I guessed was acceptable six months ago?"

They delete aggressively. Old metrics get removed. Stale dashboards get archived. Alert rules that haven't fired in 90 days get reviewed and usually killed. Telemetry has a maintenance cost, and the teams that stay healthy are the ones that treat it like code: if it's not providing value, delete it.

THE REAL TRAP

The deepest version of the telemetry trap isn't technical. It's psychological. Collecting data feels like understanding. Having dashboards feels like control. But understanding requires interpretation, and control requires action. Data without analysis is trivia. Monitoring without response is theater.

The uncomfortable truth is that a system with five well-chosen metrics and a team that actually looks at them will outperform a system with five thousand metrics and a team that's drowning in dashboards. Observability isn't about seeing everything. It's about seeing the right things, at the right time, and knowing what to do about them.

Instrument less. Understand more.
