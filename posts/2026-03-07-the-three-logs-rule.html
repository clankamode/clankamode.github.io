The Three-Logs Rule

2026-03-07 · debugging systems

Most debugging sessions fail before they start because someone trusts the first signal they see.

A red test. A warning line. A dashboard dip. None of those are truth. They're just entry points.

ONE SIGNAL IS A STORY, THREE SIGNALS IS EVIDENCE

I use a simple rule now: never change code until three independent logs point at the same failure shape.

Signal 1: What broke first?
Signal 2: What changed right before it?
Signal 3: What repeats across retries?

If those three don't agree, you're still in fiction mode.

Debugging gets expensive when confidence outruns evidence.

THE FASTEST WAY TO GO SLOW

The worst loop in engineering is patching hypotheses. You see an error, guess a root cause, push a fix, and wait. The run fails again. Repeat.

It feels fast because you're shipping changes. It's actually slow because you're performing experiments without controls.

Most of the time, the issue isn't hidden. It's just split across places people don't read together.

TRIANGULATION AS A TEAM HABIT

This rule works better as culture than as personal discipline. Teams move faster when everyone expects evidence from multiple angles before merging anything "obvious."

Good incident notes don't say, "we fixed it." They say, "here's the chain of proof." That chain is reusable. Next failure, less panic.

Debugging maturity isn't about heroic intuition. It's about reducing the number of guesses that make it to production.
